What's Actually in a Plumbing Quote?

A properly itemised plumbing quote should break down into several components. If yours is just a single number with no detail, that's your first warning sign.

Call-out or inspection fee

Typically $60 to $120. This covers the plumber's travel time and initial assessment. Many plumbers waive this if you proceed with the work. If you're being charged $150+ just for someone to show up, ask why.

Labour

This should show either the hourly rate multiplied by estimated hours, or a fixed labour charge. Look for the maths to add up ‚Äî if they're quoting 6 hours for a job that should take 2, that's a question worth asking.

Materials

Parts, pipes, fittings, fixtures. A good quote will list these individually. Watch for vague line items like "sundries" or "miscellaneous materials" over $50 ‚Äî these should be itemised.

GST

All quotes should clearly state whether they're GST-inclusive or exclusive. If the plumber has an ABN and the job is over $82.50, GST applies at 10%.

5 Signs Your Plumbing Quote Might Be Too High

1. No itemised breakdown

A single number with no detail is a red flag. You should be able to see what you're paying for labour, materials, and any other charges separately.

2. Vague "sundries" or "miscellaneous" charges

Small consumables like tape, sealant and screws are normal ‚Äî but they should cost $20 to $50 at most. If sundries are over $50, ask for an itemised list.

3. Labour hours that seem excessive

A leaking tap shouldn't take 4 hours. A toilet replacement shouldn't take a full day. If the labour hours seem high, ask the plumber to explain the complexity.

4. Premium charged for standard materials

If you're being quoted $800 for a standard mixer tap that retails for $200 at Bunnings, the markup is excessive. A reasonable markup on materials is 10-30%.

5. No licence or insurance details

A licensed plumber who carries proper insurance has overheads ‚Äî but they also provide guarantees. An unlicensed operator might be cheaper upfront and far more expensive if something goes wrong.

Questions to Ask Before You Accept

You don't need to be confrontational. These are reasonable questions that any professional plumber should be happy to answer:

"Can you break down the labour and materials separately?" ‚Äî This is the single most important question. It lets you see where the money's going.

"How many hours do you estimate this will take?" ‚Äî Helps you check if the labour charge is reasonable against the hourly rate.

"Is the call-out fee included or on top?" ‚Äî Avoids a surprise $100 added to the final bill.

"What warranty do you provide on the work?" ‚Äî A confident plumber will offer at least 12 months on workmanship.

"Can I supply my own fixtures?" ‚Äî For things like taps or toilet suites, buying them yourself can save 20-40% on the materials component.
